# Finding the Break-Even Point: A Lifecycle Framework for Evaluating Force Main Profile Tradeoffs

## Introduction

In Part 1 of this series, I discussed why the decision to remain deep or install additional air release valves extends well beyond hydraulic calculations.

While both approaches may satisfy the hydraulic requirements of the system, they often differ significantly in construction cost, maintenance requirements, operational complexity, and long-term ownership costs.

That naturally leads to the next question:

> **Can these competing costs be evaluated in a structured way?**

The answer is yes, but probably not in the way many engineers expect.

The objective is not to develop a universal equation that produces the "correct" design. Every project is different.

Instead, the goal is to build a framework that helps engineers understand the economic consequences of each alternative.

---

## There Is No Universal Break-Even Point

Every force main project is unique.

The economics of remaining deep versus returning to grade are influenced by numerous project-specific variables, including:

* Pipe diameter
* Pipe material
* Groundwater elevation
* Soil conditions
* Utility congestion
* Maintenance of Traffic (MOT) requirements
* Local construction costs
* Utility maintenance philosophy
* Emergency repair capabilities

A solution that makes perfect sense on one project may be completely inappropriate on another.

Rather than searching for a universal answer, engineers should focus on developing a consistent evaluation process.

---

## Step 1: Estimate the Cost of Staying Deep

The first step is identifying the additional costs associated with maintaining a deeper pipeline profile.

These costs extend beyond simply excavating a deeper trench.

Depending on the project, additional depth may increase:

* Excavation quantities
* Imported backfill
* Trench safety requirements
* Dewatering operations
* Construction duration
* Utility conflicts
* Contractor productivity impacts
* Maintenance of Traffic (MOT)

In coastal environments, dewatering alone may become one of the largest contributors to additional construction cost.

Although each item can often be estimated independently, collectively they represent the true cost of remaining deep.

---

## Step 2: Estimate the Lifecycle Cost of an Additional Air Release Valve

Returning the pipeline to a shallower profile reduces many construction costs, but introduces a different set of long-term obligations.

An additional air release valve represents far more than the cost of the valve itself.

Its lifecycle cost may include:

* Valve procurement
* Vault construction
* Installation
* Site access improvements
* Routine inspection
* Preventative maintenance
* Component replacement
* Complete valve replacement
* Traffic control during future maintenance

These costs may occur over several decades and should be considered as part of the overall ownership cost rather than simply the initial construction budget.

---

## Step 3: Consider Future Repair Costs

One factor that is often overlooked is the cost of future repairs.

Construction occurs once.

Maintenance continues for the life of the asset.

A deeper force main may require:

* Larger excavation areas
* Additional shoring
* More extensive dewatering
* Longer repair durations
* Greater traffic impacts
* Specialty excavation contractors

By comparison, a shallower force main generally allows repairs to be completed more quickly with fewer construction impacts.

These future costs are difficult to predict precisely, but ignoring them can significantly underestimate the true cost of ownership.

---

## Step 4: Design for the Owner

One of the most overlooked aspects of infrastructure design is recognizing that every utility operates differently.

A large metropolitan utility may have:

* Dedicated maintenance crews
* Specialized excavation equipment
* Established valve maintenance programs
* Greater internal resources

A smaller municipality may rely heavily on outside contractors and have limited staff available for routine maintenance.

Neither approach is inherently better.

However, those operational differences should influence design decisions.

The best solution is not necessarily the one with the lowest construction cost.

It is often the one that best aligns with the owner's long-term capabilities and maintenance philosophy.

---

## Bringing the Costs Together

Once the major cost components have been identified, the decision becomes much easier to visualize.

Conceptually, the lifecycle cost of remaining deep can be thought of as:

_**Deep Profile Lifecycle Cost**

= Construction Cost + Expected Future Repair Cost

Likewise, the lifecycle cost of returning to grade becomes:

_**ARV Lifecycle Cost**

= Installation + Maintenance + Replacement

The break-even point occurs where these two lifecycle costs become approximately equal.

Beyond that point, one alternative begins to provide greater long-term value than the other.

The exact location of that crossover point will vary from project to project, but the concept remains the same.

---

## A Conceptual Example

Consider a simplified example involving a 12-inch PVC force main.

Suppose avoiding a localized high point requires maintaining an additional four feet of depth for several hundred feet.

Representative assumptions might look like this:

| Cost Item                         |       Assumed Value |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------: |
| Additional deep construction cost | $50 per linear foot |
| Lifecycle cost of one ARV         |             $25,000 |

Using these representative values:

_**Break-Even Length**

= ARV Lifecycle Cost ÷ Additional Deep Construction Cost

= $25,000 ÷ $50/LF

= **500 linear feet**

Under this simplified example:

* If remaining deep requires less than approximately 500 feet of additional deep construction, it may represent the lower lifecycle cost.
* If avoiding the high point requires substantially more than 500 feet of additional deep installation, installing an additional air release valve may become the more economical alternative.

These numbers are purely illustrative.

Actual projects require project-specific cost estimates, maintenance assumptions, and engineering judgment.

The value of the exercise is not the answer itself.

It is understanding how the answer is developed.

---

## What the Numbers Don't Capture

Even the best lifecycle analysis cannot account for every consideration.

Some factors remain difficult to quantify, including:

* Hydrogen sulfide accumulation
* Odor concerns
* Utility staffing levels
* Emergency response capabilities
* Public disruption during repairs
* Risk tolerance
* Reliability objectives

Engineering judgment remains essential.

The framework simply provides better information upon which that judgment can be based.

---

## Looking Toward Better Decision Support

Today, many infrastructure decisions rely primarily on engineering experience, historical practice, and professional judgment.

Those will always remain important.

However, as utilities continue collecting better construction cost data, maintenance histories, repair records, and asset performance information, engineers have an opportunity to evaluate these tradeoffs more consistently.

Rather than replacing engineering judgment, better data can strengthen it.

Structured evaluation frameworks help ensure that important lifecycle considerations are discussed explicitly instead of remaining implicit assumptions during design.

That represents an important step toward more informed infrastructure decision-making.

---

## Final Thoughts

There is rarely a single "correct" answer in engineering.

Every project presents its own combination of technical requirements, operational realities, financial constraints, and owner preferences.

A break-even analysis does not eliminate engineering judgment.

It simply provides another tool for understanding the long-term consequences of the decisions we make.

Ultimately, that is what good engineering is about.

Not replacing experience with equations.

But combining experience, sound engineering principles, and better information to make decisions that provide the greatest long-term value.
