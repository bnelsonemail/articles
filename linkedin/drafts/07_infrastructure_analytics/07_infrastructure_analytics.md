# Utilities don't have a data problem. They have a decision problem.

After 25 years in municipal engineering, I've come to appreciate that one of the biggest challenges isn't simply finding leaks or identifying failing infrastructure. It's deciding where to focus limited time, staff, and funding before small issues become major problems.

For decades, those decisions have relied on engineering judgment, field observations, inspections, and customer reports. Those approaches remain indispensable, but they don't always reveal subtle changes happening throughout the system.

What's changing is the amount of operational data available.

Continuous monitoring of pressure, flow, tank levels, pump performance, and other operational data gives utilities a much clearer understanding of how their systems actually behave over time. Instead of relying solely on periodic inspections or reacting after failures occur, engineers can begin identifying subtle changes, emerging trends, and abnormal operating conditions that deserve investigation before they become emergencies.

To me, this is where infrastructure analytics becomes valuable.

It's not about replacing engineering judgment. It's about combining engineering experience with operational data, statistics, and predictive analytics to help answer questions like:

* Which assets are beginning to behave differently?
* Where should field investigations be prioritized?
* Which parts of the system present the greatest operational risk?
* Where will limited maintenance and capital dollars have the greatest impact?

I believe the future of our industry is moving in this direction. As utilities continue to collect more operational data, the real opportunity isn't simply monitoring systems. It's transforming that data into actionable engineering insight that helps us better understand infrastructure, reduce uncertainty, and make more informed decisions before failures occur.

Infrastructure analytics isn't about predicting the future with certainty. It's about reducing uncertainty so engineers can make better decisions.
