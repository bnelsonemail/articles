# Force Main Air Release Valve Placement: Deep Run vs. Additional Maintenance Points

During the design of a large-diameter wastewater force main, several utility crossings required the pipeline profile to dip beneath existing infrastructure before returning to grade.

Individually, each crossing could be addressed.

But when the crossings were evaluated together, the profile created a broader design question. Each dip and return to grade introduced the potential for localized high points along the force main.

The question became straightforward:

Should the pipeline remain deeper for an extended distance to avoid additional high points, or should the design accept a shallower profile and rely on additional air release valves?

At first glance, this may seem like a simple profile adjustment. In reality, it is a lifecycle design decision involving hydraulics, construction cost, long-term maintenance, odor control, corrosion risk, utility access, and owner preferences.

The best answer is rarely obvious.

---

## Why High Points Matter in Force Mains

High points in force mains are important because air and gases naturally migrate to elevated locations within the pipeline profile.

In a pressurized system, trapped air can create several operational problems. It can reduce the effective cross-sectional area of the pipe, increase headloss, reduce hydraulic efficiency, affect pump performance, and contribute to transient pressure concerns.

For wastewater force mains, the issue can be more complicated. Gases accumulating at high points may contain hydrogen sulfide, which can contribute to odor concerns and corrosion. If gas pockets persist, the long-term condition of the pipe and related appurtenances may be affected.

This is why air release valves are commonly installed at high points in force mains. Their purpose is to help remove accumulated air or gas from the system so the pipeline can continue operating as intended.

But every ARV also becomes an asset.

It must be located, installed, protected, accessed, inspected, and maintained. In wastewater service, ARVs can also introduce odor control and corrosion considerations. They are not simply small details on a plan sheet. They are long-term maintenance points.

---

## The Design Decision Created by Utility Crossings

Utility crossings often create profile constraints.

A proposed force main may need to pass beneath existing water mains, storm drains, gravity sewers, reclaimed water mains, gas mains, communication lines, or other infrastructure. Each crossing may require a localized dip in the proposed pipe profile.

If the pipe dips under a crossing and then returns to a shallower depth, a high point may be created before the next crossing. Where several crossings occur over a relatively short distance, the profile may develop multiple localized high points.

That creates two basic design approaches.

One option is to maintain a deeper continuous profile through the conflict area, avoiding intermediate high points.

The other option is to keep the pipe shallower where possible and install ARVs at the resulting high points.

Neither option is perfect.

The decision becomes a tradeoff between construction complexity and long-term operational complexity.

---

## Option 1: Deeper Continuous Profile with Fewer High Points

A deeper continuous profile lowers the force main beneath the first conflict and maintains a deeper alignment through the utility crossing area. Instead of rising back up between crossings, the pipe remains lower until it can return to a shallower depth without creating additional high points.

### Advantages

The primary advantage is that a deeper continuous profile may reduce the number of localized high points.

Fewer high points generally means fewer ARVs. That can reduce the number of appurtenances requiring inspection, maintenance, odor control, access coordination, and eventual replacement.

From a utility operations perspective, this may be attractive. Fewer ARVs can simplify long-term maintenance and reduce the number of locations where air or gases must be managed.

This approach can also reduce the potential for repeated gas accumulation points along the alignment. For wastewater force mains, reducing unnecessary gas collection locations may help reduce odor and corrosion concerns over the life of the asset.

### Disadvantages

The deeper profile is not free.

A deeper force main can significantly increase construction complexity. Additional excavation depth may require more shoring, more dewatering, larger trench boxes, additional safety considerations, and more difficult utility coordination.

In areas with high groundwater, poor soils, narrow rights-of-way, or heavy traffic, a deeper installation can quickly become more expensive and more difficult to construct.

Future repairs may also become more complicated. A force main installed at greater depth may require more extensive excavation, traffic control, dewatering, and restoration if repair work is needed later.

The deeper option may reduce the number of ARVs, but it can shift risk into construction and future repair activities.

---

## Option 2: Shallower Profile with Additional ARVs

A shallower profile allows the force main to rise back toward typical cover between utility crossings. This can reduce excavation depth and make construction more straightforward.

However, when the pipe rises between crossings, localized high points may be created. Those high points typically require ARVs.

### Advantages

The primary advantage of the shallower option is reduced construction depth.

Shallower installation may reduce excavation volume, shoring requirements, dewatering needs, and construction risk. It may also simplify future access to the pipeline if repairs are needed.

From a capital cost perspective, this option may appear more attractive during design and bidding. Less excavation usually means less cost, less time, and fewer construction complications.

In constrained urban corridors, a shallower profile may also reduce impacts to traffic, nearby utilities, businesses, and residents.

### Disadvantages

The disadvantage is that the utility may inherit more maintenance points.

Each additional ARV becomes another asset that must be inspected and maintained. Depending on location, ARV maintenance may require traffic control, access considerations, odor management, corrosion-resistant materials, and periodic replacement.

Additional ARVs also increase the number of appurtenances that can fail, clog, leak, corrode, or become inaccessible over time.

A shallower profile may be easier to build, but it may not be easier to own.

---

## Additional Factors Influencing the Decision

The right answer depends on more than the pipe profile.

A deeper continuous run may be preferred on one project, while additional ARVs may be the better choice on another. The decision should be based on site-specific conditions, utility preferences, and lifecycle considerations.

### Construction Environment

Urban construction can make deep excavation especially challenging.

Traffic control, limited working space, utility congestion, access restrictions, nearby structures, and impacts to businesses or residents can all increase the cost and risk of deeper installation.

In some corridors, the difference between a moderately deep force main and a very deep force main may be substantial.

### Groundwater Conditions

Groundwater can significantly influence the decision.

A deeper profile may require more extensive dewatering, which can increase construction cost, schedule risk, permitting considerations, and potential settlement concerns.

In areas with high groundwater, the cost of avoiding additional high points may need to be weighed carefully against the long-term maintenance burden of additional ARVs.

### Utility Owner Preferences

Different utilities may approach this tradeoff differently.

Some owners may prefer minimizing ARVs because they want fewer long-term maintenance obligations. Others may prefer shallower construction and accept additional ARVs if their maintenance staff can reliably inspect and service them.

Owner preference matters because the owner is ultimately responsible for operating and maintaining the system long after construction is complete.

### Maintenance Capabilities

Maintenance capability should be considered during design.

A utility with adequate staffing, equipment, access procedures, and maintenance history may be more comfortable with additional ARVs. A utility with limited staff or difficult access conditions may prefer to reduce the number of appurtenances where possible.

This is not only a design question. It is an operations question.

### Accessibility

Accessibility should be considered over the entire life of the asset.

A deep pipeline may be harder and more expensive to repair. However, additional ARVs may require routine access, periodic maintenance, and safe working conditions.

ARV locations should also be selected carefully. In wastewater applications, ARVs should be located where they can be protected, accessed, and maintained. They should also be installed in locations protected from flooding so that, during vacuum conditions, the valve admits air rather than floodwater.

### Easement and Right-of-Way Constraints

Available easement width, existing utility corridors, property constraints, and future development should also be considered.

Even if a deeper profile is hydraulically attractive, it may not be practical if the available corridor cannot support the required construction methods. Similarly, adding ARVs may not be ideal if access to those locations will be difficult or unsafe.

---

## The Lifecycle Tradeoff

The real tradeoff is not simply deep pipe versus shallow pipe.

It is a balance between:

* Initial construction cost and long-term maintenance cost
* Construction complexity and operational complexity
* Reduced excavation and increased appurtenances
* Capital expenditure and lifecycle cost
* Contractor convenience and utility maintenance burden
* Short-term constructability and long-term reliability

A design that is easier to build may create more maintenance obligations. A design that simplifies operations may increase construction cost and risk.

Neither outcome is automatically wrong.

The important question is whether the tradeoff is understood, documented, and acceptable to the utility owner.

---

## Practical Design Takeaways

Several practical lessons come from this type of force main profile decision.

First, high points should not be treated as minor drafting details. In force mains, high points can create real hydraulic and operational concerns.

Second, ARVs should be viewed as long-term assets, not just plan sheet symbols. Each valve requires access, protection, inspection, and maintenance.

Third, deeper profiles can reduce the number of high points, but the cost and risk of deeper construction must be evaluated honestly.

Fourth, utility operations staff should be part of the conversation. The people maintaining the system may have a very different view of the tradeoff than the people bidding the project.

Finally, the preferred alternative should be documented. If a shallower profile with additional ARVs is selected, the owner should understand the maintenance implications. If a deeper profile is selected, the owner should understand the construction and future repair implications.

Good design is not just about getting the pipe in the ground.

It is about creating an asset the utility can operate, maintain, and afford over its service life.

---

## Conclusion

There is rarely a single correct answer when utility crossings create high points in a force main.

A deeper continuous profile may reduce localized high points and limit the number of ARVs, but it can increase excavation depth, construction cost, dewatering requirements, and future repair complexity.

A shallower profile may reduce construction impacts, but it can create additional high points and increase long-term maintenance obligations.

The optimal solution depends on site conditions, utility owner preferences, groundwater, construction constraints, maintenance capabilities, accessibility, and lifecycle cost.

Ultimately, the goal is not simply to design a force main that can be constructed.

The goal is to design a force main that can be operated and maintained reliably for decades.

That requires more than following a profile. It requires engineering judgment.
