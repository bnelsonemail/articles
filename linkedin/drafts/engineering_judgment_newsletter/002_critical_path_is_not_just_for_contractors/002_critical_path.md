# The Critical Path Isn't Just for Contractors

When most engineers hear the phrase *critical path*, they immediately think of construction.

Concrete placements. Pipe installation. Equipment delivery. Commissioning.

Those activities are certainly important, but one of the biggest lessons I've learned over the course of my career is that the critical path often begins long before construction crews arrive on site.

In many infrastructure projects, the schedule is already being won or lost during design.

That's because the critical path isn't simply a contractor's concern. It's an engineering concern.

A project schedule is much more than a list of activities and dates. It's a map of dependencies. It reveals which decisions must happen before others can move forward, where delays are likely to occur, and which activities have little or no flexibility. Understanding those relationships allows engineers to identify risks early, make informed decisions, and keep projects moving.

Some of the activities that frequently drive the critical path have nothing to do with construction itself.

* Owner review and approval cycles
* Environmental permitting
* Utility coordination
* Easement acquisition
* Long-lead equipment procurement
* Regulatory agency reviews

Any one of these activities can delay a project weeks or even months before the first piece of equipment is mobilized.

I've seen projects where engineering design was completed on time, yet construction was delayed because permits took longer than expected. I've seen owner decisions postpone major milestones because a seemingly minor design question couldn't be resolved. I've also seen opportunities where identifying a dependency early allowed the team to restructure the schedule, perform work in parallel, and avoid significant delays.

That's where engineering judgment becomes just as important as project management.

Scheduling software can calculate the critical path automatically as activities and dates change.

That part is easy.

The difficult part is understanding which activities deserve the most attention before they become schedule problems.

Consider permitting.

Many regulatory agencies require several weeks simply to complete an initial review. If comments are issued, the engineer must revise the plans, address every comment thoroughly, and resubmit the package. The revised plans often enter another review queue, adding several more weeks before work can continue.

One overlooked permit comment isn't just a small mistake.

It can easily become a thirty-day schedule delay.

Experienced engineers understand that the objective isn't merely responding to comments. It's providing a complete, coordinated response that avoids another review cycle altogether.

Timing is equally important.

Submitting permit documents too early may result in incomplete information that generates unnecessary comments. Waiting too long may leave the project sitting in a review queue while every downstream activity waits. Knowing when a design package is mature enough to submit is often just as important as completing the design itself.

The same principle applies throughout a project.

Owner review meetings don't happen the day drawings are completed. Utility companies have their own review timelines. Equipment manufacturers have production schedules and long lead times. Agency coordination depends on the availability of people outside your organization.

Each introduces uncertainty that cannot simply be compressed later.

Good schedules recognize these realities instead of assuming everything proceeds exactly as planned.

Even internal staffing decisions affect the critical path.

A senior designer with years of experience may complete a complex design task significantly faster than a junior engineer who is still developing those skills. Likewise, assigning too many critical activities to one person can create a bottleneck that delays the entire project.

Developing a realistic schedule requires understanding not only the work itself, but also the capabilities and availability of the people performing it.

That's engineering judgment.

Another concept that experienced engineers learn to appreciate is schedule float.

Not every delay affects the project completion date.

Activities with available float provide flexibility. Critical path activities typically do not.

Understanding the difference allows project teams to focus their attention where it matters most. Spending days recovering a minor delay on an activity with several weeks of float may accomplish very little. Overlooking a single critical-path activity, however, can delay the entire project.

As infrastructure projects become larger and more complex, data and analytics are playing an increasingly important role in schedule management. Modern scheduling tools can evaluate alternative sequences, model uncertainty, identify resource conflicts, and highlight activities that present the greatest schedule risk.

Those tools are incredibly valuable.

But they don't replace engineering judgment.

Analytics can identify where risk exists.

Engineers decide how to respond.

The best project schedules don't simply predict completion dates.

They help teams understand where the greatest risks exist, where coordination matters most, and where proactive planning can prevent costly delays before construction ever begins.

The best engineers don't use schedules simply to track progress.

They use them to anticipate problems, communicate priorities, and make better decisions.

That's why understanding the critical path isn't just a project management skill.

It's an engineering skill.
