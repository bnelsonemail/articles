# The Critical Path Isn't Just for Contractors

When most engineers hear the phrase *critical path*, they immediately think about construction.

* Concrete placements
* Pipe installation
* Equipment delivery
* Commissioning

Those activities are certainly important, but after more than two decades working on municipal water and wastewater infrastructure, I've learned that the critical path often begins long before a contractor ever mobilizes equipment to the site.

In many infrastructure projects, the schedule is already being won or lost during design.

The critical path is not simply a contractor's concern.

It is an engineering concern.

---

## More Than a Construction Schedule

At its core, a project schedule is a network of dependencies.

Every activity relies on something else being completed first. Some tasks have flexibility. Others have none. The critical path identifies the sequence of activities that directly controls the project's completion date. Delay one of those activities, and the entire project moves with it.

Modern scheduling software makes identifying the critical path almost effortless.

Understanding *why* certain activities belong on it is considerably more difficult.

That requires engineering judgment.

Experienced engineers recognize that many of the activities most likely to drive the schedule occur months before construction begins.

Typical examples include:

* Environmental permitting
* Regulatory agency reviews
* Owner review and approval cycles
* Utility coordination
* Easement acquisition
* Survey and geotechnical investigations
* Long-lead equipment procurement
* Property access agreements

None of these activities involve pouring concrete or installing pipe.

Yet any one of them can delay construction by weeks or even months.

---

## Design Decisions Shape the Schedule

Engineering is often viewed as producing drawings and specifications.

In reality, design is also about making decisions at the right time.

I've worked on projects where the engineering design itself finished on schedule, but construction started months late because permits remained under review.

I've also seen projects delayed because an owner decision on what initially appeared to be a minor design issue prevented the rest of the design from moving forward.

Conversely, I've seen teams identify a critical dependency early enough to restructure the schedule, perform activities in parallel, and eliminate months of unnecessary delay.

Those opportunities rarely appear by accident.

They come from understanding how technical decisions influence the overall project schedule.

---

## Permitting Is More Than Paperwork

Permitting is one of the clearest examples of engineering judgment affecting the critical path.

Most regulatory agencies require several weeks simply to complete an initial review.

If comments are issued, engineers must:

* Review every agency comment carefully.
* Revise the design where necessary.
* Prepare written responses.
* Coordinate revisions across multiple disciplines.
* Resubmit the package.

The revised plans typically enter another review queue before approval.

A single overlooked comment can easily become a thirty-day schedule delay.

Experienced engineers understand that success isn't measured by responding to comments.

It's measured by avoiding the second review cycle whenever possible.

That requires complete, coordinated, and technically sound responses.

---

## Timing Matters

Submitting a permit package too early can be just as problematic as submitting it too late.

An immature design often generates unnecessary review comments because important information has not yet been developed.

Waiting too long creates a different problem.

The project simply sits in the agency's review queue while every downstream activity waits.

Knowing **when** a design package is sufficiently mature for review is one of those decisions that no scheduling software can make.

That comes from experience.

---

## Coordination Creates the Real Critical Path

Infrastructure projects rarely operate within a single organization.

Successful projects require coordination between numerous independent groups, each with its own priorities and review timelines.

These frequently include:

* Utility companies
* Environmental agencies
* Transportation departments
* Property owners
* Equipment manufacturers
* Surveyors
* Geotechnical consultants
* Contractors
* Municipal review staff

Every organization introduces uncertainty.

Every review cycle consumes time.

Every dependency has the potential to become part of the critical path.

Good project schedules acknowledge those realities instead of assuming every review will occur exactly as planned.

---

## Resources Matter Too

The critical path is influenced not only by activities but also by the people performing them.

A senior engineer with years of design experience may complete a complex hydraulic analysis in a fraction of the time required by someone still developing those skills.

Likewise, assigning multiple critical activities to the same individual creates a resource bottleneck regardless of how well the schedule appears on paper.

Realistic schedules account for:

* Staff experience
* Workload balancing
* Discipline coordination
* Availability during reviews
* Organizational priorities

Ignoring resource constraints often creates schedule delays that could have been avoided during planning.

---

## Understanding Float

One of the most misunderstood concepts in scheduling is float.

Not every delayed activity delays the project.

Some tasks have schedule flexibility.

Others do not.

Understanding float allows project managers and engineers to prioritize their attention where it provides the greatest benefit.

Recovering two days on an activity with several weeks of float may have little impact on the overall project.

Losing two days on a true critical-path activity may shift every remaining milestone.

Engineering judgment helps determine which delays deserve immediate attention and which simply require monitoring.

---

## Where Analytics Fits

As infrastructure projects become larger and more complex, schedule analytics are becoming increasingly valuable.

Modern software can:

* Model alternative construction sequences.
* Simulate schedule uncertainty.
* Identify resource conflicts.
* Evaluate schedule risk.
* Predict potential delays.
* Quantify the likelihood of meeting milestone dates.

These capabilities provide tremendous insight.

But analytics alone cannot determine the best course of action.

Analytics identify where risk exists.

Engineers determine how that risk should be managed.

The future of project delivery will increasingly combine engineering experience with data-driven decision support, allowing teams to identify schedule problems earlier and make better-informed decisions before delays occur.

---

## Engineering Judgment Drives the Schedule

The best schedules do more than predict completion dates.

They help project teams understand where coordination matters most, where uncertainty exists, and where proactive planning can eliminate costly delays before construction begins.

The best engineers don't use schedules simply to track progress.

They use them to anticipate problems, communicate priorities, allocate resources effectively, and improve decision-making throughout the life of a project.

Understanding the critical path is not just a project management skill.

It is an engineering skill.

As infrastructure projects continue to grow in complexity, the engineers who can combine technical expertise, project management, and data analytics will be best equipped to deliver projects successfully.

Because the critical path doesn't begin when construction starts.

More often than not, it begins with engineering.

---

## Closing Thoughts

Every schedule tells a story.

Some simply document when work is expected to occur.

The best schedules explain *why* certain activities matter, where uncertainty exists, and how thoughtful planning can prevent small issues from becoming major delays.

Engineering judgment remains the foundation of that process.

The software may calculate the critical path.

The engineer determines what to do about it.

---

**Engineering begins with calculations.**  
**Infrastructure analytics powers better decisions.**
