# The Most Common TDH Mistake I See

After years of designing lift stations and force mains, one mistake seems to come up more often than almost any other.

People focus on the **elevation difference** between the wet well and the discharge point.

They forget about the **pressure**.

Total Dynamic Head (TDH) isn't just the vertical lift.

It's the total energy the pump must overcome, including:

- Static head
- Friction losses in the force main and fittings
- Pressure requirements at the discharge point

I've seen calculations that looked perfectly reasonable until someone realized the pipeline discharged into a system operating at a significant pressure.

That discharge pressure can represent tens or even hundreds of feet of additional head.

Ignoring it doesn't just make the math wrong.

It can result in selecting a pump that never operates where you expected.

For example, a force main discharging into a system with 40 psi of pressure adds approximately 92 feet of head before friction losses are even considered. If that pressure is overlooked during pump selection, the chosen pump may never operate near its intended duty point.

One of the things I enjoy most about hydraulic design is that the equations are usually the easy part.

Understanding **which terms belong in the equation** is where engineering judgment comes in.

The calculation is only as good as the assumptions behind it.
