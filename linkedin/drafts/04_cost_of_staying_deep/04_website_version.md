# The Cost of Staying Deep: When Force Main Design Becomes an Economic Decision

## Introduction

In my previous article, I discussed a force main design where several utility crossings required the pipeline profile to dip beneath existing infrastructure before returning to grade. Individually, none of the crossings presented a significant challenge. Together, however, they created a much larger design question.

> Should the pipeline remain deeper for an extended distance to eliminate localized high points, or should it return to a shallower profile and rely on additional air release valves?

This article is the first in a two-part series exploring that decision.

I'll begin by briefly revisiting the original design problem before expanding the discussion into the construction, operational, and lifecycle tradeoffs that influence the decision. In next week's article, I'll build on that discussion by introducing a conceptual framework for evaluating where the economic break-even point may occur between the two alternatives.

From a hydraulic perspective, either solution could work.

The more difficult question had little to do with hydraulics.

It was an economic decision.

This is one of those situations where engineering extends beyond calculations and design standards. The hydraulic grade line may indicate that either option is acceptable, but determining which option provides the greatest long-term value requires considering far more than flow and pressure.

Construction cost, future maintenance, operational complexity, repairability, and utility capabilities all become part of the discussion.

For many infrastructure projects, these broader considerations ultimately drive the final design.

---

## A Problem Without a Universal Answer

At first glance, the decision appears straightforward.

Avoid the high points and eliminate the need for additional air release valves.

Or return to grade, install the valves, and reduce construction costs.

In reality, neither approach is inherently better.

Every project presents a unique combination of site conditions, construction challenges, operational requirements, and owner preferences. What represents the most economical solution on one project may be the more expensive choice on another.

That is one of the reasons these decisions remain interesting even after decades of engineering practice.

Experience helps identify the tradeoffs, but it rarely eliminates them.

---

## Option One: Staying Deep

Maintaining a deeper pipeline profile can offer several operational advantages.

By remaining below the utility crossings, the pipeline avoids creating additional localized high points where air may accumulate. Fewer high points generally translate into fewer air release valves, fewer above-grade appurtenances, and fewer components requiring inspection and maintenance throughout the life of the system.

For wastewater force mains, reducing the number of high points may also help minimize locations where hydrogen sulfide can accumulate, potentially reducing odor concerns and long-term corrosion risks.

These are meaningful benefits.

They are also only part of the equation.

As pipeline depth increases, excavation volumes grow, trench safety requirements become more significant, groundwater management becomes more difficult, and construction productivity often decreases.

Future repairs become more challenging as well.

A design choice made to simplify operations today may increase the cost and complexity of emergency repairs decades from now.

---

## Option Two: Returning to Grade

Returning the pipeline to a shallower profile after each utility crossing changes the balance.

Construction generally becomes simpler.

Excavation quantities decrease.

Dewatering requirements are often reduced.

Maintenance of traffic durations may be shortened.

Future repairs typically become easier because the infrastructure is more accessible.

These are substantial advantages, particularly in urban environments where excavation depth can significantly influence construction cost.

However, this approach introduces additional infrastructure that must be owned and maintained.

Each air release valve represents another asset requiring periodic inspection, maintenance, eventual replacement, and ongoing operational attention.

For utilities with well-established maintenance programs, this additional workload may be relatively minor.

For others, it can represent a meaningful long-term commitment.

---

## Looking Beyond Construction Cost

It is tempting to compare only the contractor's bid price.

After all, construction costs are the most visible numbers during design.

They are also the easiest to quantify.

But infrastructure often remains in service for fifty years or more.

Over that period, utilities may perform routine maintenance, emergency repairs, system upgrades, and asset replacements that were never reflected in the original construction estimate.

A deeper force main may save maintenance costs associated with additional air release valves while increasing the cost of every future excavation.

Conversely, a shallower pipeline may reduce repair complexity while introducing decades of valve inspections, maintenance, and eventual replacement.

Neither cost appears entirely on bid day.

Both contribute to the total cost of ownership.

---

## Engineering Judgment Beyond the Calculations

One of the aspects I enjoy most about municipal engineering is that many important design decisions cannot be solved by a single equation.

Hydraulic calculations establish what is technically acceptable.

Engineering judgment helps determine what is practically desirable.

The best solution often depends on factors that are difficult to quantify.

How experienced are the utility's maintenance crews?

Does the utility own excavation equipment capable of performing deep emergency repairs?

How disruptive would prolonged lane closures be in this location?

How likely are future utility conflicts?

Questions like these rarely appear in a design manual, yet they often influence the outcome just as much as hydraulic calculations.

---

## The Bigger Lesson

This force main example illustrates a broader principle that extends well beyond pipeline design.

Many infrastructure decisions are not purely technical problems.

They are economic, operational, and lifecycle decisions.

The objective is rarely to identify a universally correct answer.

Instead, the goal is to understand the tradeoffs well enough to select the solution that provides the greatest long-term value for a particular utility and a particular project.

That process requires both engineering analysis and engineering judgment.

Neither can replace the other.

## Looking Ahead: Part Two

One question still remains.

**If both approaches involve costs that extend well beyond construction, how can those costs be compared in a structured and meaningful way?**

Is there a point where remaining deep becomes more expensive than installing another air release valve?

Or, viewed another way, is there a break-even point where the economics begin to favor one approach over the other?

Those questions deserve more than intuition.

In **Part Two**, which I'll publish next week, I'll introduce a conceptual framework for evaluating this tradeoff, including a simple break-even approach that illustrates how engineers can begin comparing the lifecycle costs of remaining deep versus installing additional air release valves.

Rather than searching for a universal answer, the goal is to build a structured way to think about these decisions and better understand the long-term consequences of each alternative.

Because good engineering isn't about finding a single "correct" answer.

It's about understanding the tradeoffs well enough to make better decisions.
