# Lift Station Reliability: Engineering Beyond the Pump

## Introduction

When discussing lift station reliability, it's easy to focus on the most visible piece of equipment: the pumps.

Pump selection is certainly important. Engineers spend considerable effort evaluating pump curves, efficiencies, operating points, and total dynamic head to ensure a station performs as intended.

But after more than 25 years working in water and wastewater engineering, I've come to believe that reliable lift stations are rarely the result of a single component.

They are the result of a well-designed system.

A lift station can have excellent pumps and still experience frequent operational problems if other critical components are overlooked. Likewise, a station with average equipment can provide decades of dependable service when the entire system has been thoughtfully designed, maintained, and operated.

Reliability is ultimately a systems engineering problem.

## Reliability Begins During Design

The foundation of reliability is established long before construction begins.

During design, engineers evaluate hydraulic performance through calculations such as:

* Design flow rates
* Total Dynamic Head (TDH)
* Pump operating curves
* Wet well storage volume
* Pump cycling frequency
* Emergency storage capacity

These analyses ensure the station can meet its required hydraulic performance.

However, hydraulic performance alone does not guarantee operational reliability.

Engineers must also consider questions that extend well beyond the calculations.

* What happens if one pump is unavailable?
* Can maintenance be performed safely and efficiently?
* Is adequate redundancy provided?
* Can operators easily access critical equipment?
* How long can the station operate during a power outage?
* What are the consequences of a prolonged equipment failure?

These considerations often have a greater impact on long-term reliability than selecting a pump with slightly higher efficiency.

## A Lift Station Is an Integrated System

Every lift station contains numerous components that work together.

Pumps are only one part of the overall system.

Other critical elements include:

* Motors
* Variable frequency drives
* Level instrumentation
* Check valves
* Isolation valves
* Force mains
* Electrical distribution
* Backup generators
* SCADA and telemetry systems
* Emergency power transfer equipment

The reliability of the station depends on how these components interact.

A failed level transmitter may prevent pumps from operating correctly.

A sticking check valve may increase pump cycling.

Poorly maintained generators may leave the station vulnerable during power outages.

Air accumulation within a force main can reduce hydraulic performance and create operational issues.

Rarely is reliability determined by a single failure.

More often, it is the cumulative effect of many small issues occurring throughout the system.

## Operations and Maintenance Matter

No design can eliminate the need for good operations and maintenance.

Routine inspection and preventive maintenance remain essential to reliable operation.

Examples include:

* Exercising isolation valves
* Testing backup generators
* Verifying level sensor calibration
* Inspecting check valves
* Monitoring excessive pump starts
* Reviewing alarm histories
* Tracking unusual changes in pump run times

Many failures provide warning signs before they become emergencies.

The challenge is recognizing those warning signs early enough to act.

## The Growing Role of Operational Data

Modern utilities collect far more operational data than ever before.

SCADA systems continuously record information such as:

* Pump run times
* Starts per hour
* Wet well levels
* Power consumption
* Alarm frequency
* Generator operation
* Maintenance history

For many utilities, this data is primarily used to answer operational questions.

* Is the station running?
* Did an alarm occur?
* Did a pump trip overnight?

Those are important questions, but they only describe what is happening right now.

The greater opportunity lies in understanding what the data says about the future.

Operational trends can reveal subtle changes long before equipment fails.

A gradual increase in pump run time may indicate declining pump performance.

More frequent starts may suggest changing inflow conditions or control issues.

Rising energy consumption could point toward wear, fouling, or hydraulic changes within the system.

Individually, these observations may appear insignificant.

Together, they provide engineers with a much richer understanding of how a lift station is behaving over time.

The goal isn't simply to collect more data.

It's to make better maintenance, rehabilitation, and capital planning decisions before failures occur.

## Engineering Judgment and Infrastructure Analytics

This is where I believe infrastructure analytics becomes particularly valuable.

Engineering has always involved making decisions under uncertainty.

Experience, field observations, inspections, and engineering judgment remain essential, but they all provide only part of the picture.

Operational data adds another layer of evidence.

Statistical analysis and machine learning can identify patterns, trends, and subtle changes that may be difficult to recognize through periodic inspections alone.

That doesn't eliminate the need for engineering judgment.

It strengthens it.

Engineers still decide whether a trend is meaningful, whether additional investigation is warranted, and what corrective action should be taken.

Analytics simply helps ensure those decisions are based on more complete information.

## Final Thoughts

Reliable lift stations are not created by selecting the "best" pump.

They result from thoughtful engineering that considers the entire system, from hydraulic design and equipment redundancy to operations, maintenance, and long-term asset management.

As utilities continue collecting larger volumes of operational data, the opportunity isn't simply to monitor infrastructure more closely.

It's to make better engineering decisions.

By combining engineering experience with statistical analysis and infrastructure analytics, utilities can move beyond reacting to failures and begin understanding the conditions that lead to them.

Engineering judgment remains at the center of every important decision.

Better information simply helps engineers apply that judgment with greater confidence.
