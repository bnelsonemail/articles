# When Utility Crossings Create High Points: A Force Main Design Decision

During design of a large-diameter force main, several utility crossings required the pipeline profile to dip beneath existing infrastructure before returning to grade.

On paper, each crossing could be handled.

But when the crossings were viewed together, the profile created a bigger design question. The dips and returns to grade resulted in multiple localized high points along the force main.

The question was straightforward:

Should the pipeline remain deeper for extended distances to avoid these high points, or should the design accept the profile changes and rely on additional air release valves?

From a hydraulic perspective, the high points would require additional air release valves to help prevent air accumulation and potential flow restrictions. But each additional valve also adds cost, maintenance responsibility, access needs, and another appurtenance the utility must manage over the life of the system.

For wastewater force mains, this concern extends beyond hydraulics. Gases accumulating at high points may contain hydrogen sulfide, which can contribute to odor issues, corrosion, and long-term asset deterioration.

That is what makes this type of design decision more than just a profile adjustment.

It becomes a lifecycle tradeoff.

## Option 1: Lower the Profile

One approach is to lower the force main profile and maintain a deeper continuous run through the conflict area.

This can reduce the number of localized high points and limit the number of air release valves needed along the alignment. Fewer ARVs can mean fewer appurtenances to inspect, maintain, protect, and access over the life of the system.

From an operations standpoint, this may be attractive. It can simplify the system and reduce potential odor, corrosion, and maintenance concerns associated with repeated high points.

But the deeper profile is not free.

A deeper force main may require additional excavation, shoring, dewatering, utility coordination, and construction risk. It may also make future repairs more difficult and more expensive. In areas with high groundwater, poor soils, limited right-of-way, or heavy traffic, the cost and complexity of deeper installation can increase quickly.

## Option 2: Stay Shallower and Add ARVs

The other approach is to keep the force main profile shallower and install air release valves at the resulting high points.

This can reduce excavation depth, limit dewatering requirements, simplify construction, and make future pipe access easier. From a capital cost standpoint, this option may look more attractive during design and bidding.

But it shifts more responsibility to long-term operations and maintenance.

Each additional ARV becomes another asset to inspect and maintain. It may require traffic control, access coordination, odor control considerations, and periodic replacement. It also creates another location where the utility must manage air, gases, corrosion potential, and appurtenance reliability.

A shallower profile may be easier to build, but it may not be easier to own.

## The Real Tradeoff

This decision is not just about pipe depth.

It is a lifecycle decision.

A deeper continuous profile may reduce operational complexity but increase construction cost and risk. A shallower profile may reduce initial construction impacts but increase the number of long-term maintenance points.

The best answer depends on the specific project conditions, including:

* Existing utility conflicts
* Groundwater conditions
* Excavation depth
* Soil conditions
* Traffic control requirements
* Easement and right-of-way constraints
* Utility maintenance capabilities
* Owner preferences
* Long-term accessibility
* Lifecycle cost

This is where engineering judgment matters.

The lowest construction cost option is not always the best long-term utility asset. At the same time, eliminating every high point may not be practical if it creates excessive excavation depth, dewatering risk, or future repair challenges.

## Final Thought

Force main profile design is often a balancing act between construction practicality and long-term maintainability.

When utility crossings create high points, the design team should not only ask:

> Can we build it?

They should also ask:

> Can the utility operate and maintain it for the next several decades?

Sometimes the right answer is a deeper continuous profile.

Sometimes the right answer is a shallower profile with strategically placed air release valves.

The important part is making that decision intentionally, documenting the tradeoffs, and designing for the full life of the asset, not just the construction phase.
