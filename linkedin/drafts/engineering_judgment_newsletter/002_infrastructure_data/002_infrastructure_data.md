# What Makes Infrastructure Data Useful?

Municipal utilities have never had more data available than they do today.

SCADA systems continuously monitor pressures, flows, tank levels, pump runtimes, and energy consumption. Geographic Information Systems (GIS) track thousands of infrastructure assets. Computerized Maintenance Management Systems (CMMS) record work orders, inspections, repairs, and maintenance history. Customer service records, laboratory results, and field observations continue to grow with every passing year.

The amount of information available to utilities is enormous.

Yet one question remains:

**What actually makes all of that data useful?**

The answer isn't the amount of data being collected.

It's whether that data helps engineers and operators make better decisions.

Data, by itself, has little value. Useful data reduces uncertainty, provides context, and supports decisions that improve reliability, reduce costs, and better serve customers.

For municipalities, those decisions generally fall into two categories: understanding today's system and preparing for tomorrow's.

## The Infrastructure Decision Lifecycle

Infrastructure data becomes valuable because it supports a continuous engineering decision-making process.

It begins by understanding what is happening today. Engineers then analyze why it is happening, estimate what is likely to happen next, and ultimately determine the best course of action.

That progression can be viewed as the engineering decision lifecycle:

```text
Operational Analytics
"What is happening?"
        │
        ▼
Engineering Analysis
"Why is it happening?"
        │
        ▼
Predictive Analytics
"What is likely to happen?"
        │
        ▼
Engineering Judgment
"What should we do?"
```

Each stage builds upon the one before it.

Without operational data, there is little basis for meaningful analysis. Without engineering analysis, predictive models lack context. And without engineering judgment, even the most sophisticated analytics cannot improve infrastructure decisions.

Technology supports each step of this process, but it does not replace the engineer. Data provides evidence. Engineering transforms that evidence into better decisions.

## Operational Analytics: Understanding Today's System

Every day, operators and engineers use operational data to understand how their infrastructure is performing.

When a lift station alarms overnight, a pressure zone begins behaving unexpectedly, or a high-service pump suddenly consumes more energy than normal, operational data provides the information needed to investigate the problem.

For example, a sudden drop in pressure may indicate a water main break, an unexpected valve closure, or a malfunctioning pressure reducing valve. An increase in pump run times could indicate higher system demand, a partially blocked force main, or declining pump efficiency. Rising wet well levels at a lift station may point to a clogged pump or mechanical failure before an overflow occurs.

Operational analytics helps engineers answer questions such as:

- What is happening?
- Where is the problem occurring?
- Which assets are affected?
- How severe is the issue?
- What is the most likely cause?
- What actions should crews take?

By combining SCADA data, maintenance records, inspection reports, field observations, and engineering knowledge, municipalities can diagnose problems, dispatch crews more efficiently, restore service more quickly, and reduce the impact on customers.

Once repairs are complete, the same operational data helps verify that the problem has actually been resolved. Pressure returns to normal, pump run times stabilize, tank levels recover, and system performance can be confirmed rather than assumed.

Operational analytics is therefore more than monitoring a system.

It is about understanding infrastructure well enough to diagnose problems, repair them effectively, and verify that the system has returned to normal operation.

## Engineering Analysis: Turning Data Into Understanding

Operational data rarely provides answers by itself.

It provides evidence.

Engineering analysis is what transforms that evidence into understanding.

A pressure drop alone does not confirm a water main break. It may indicate a failed pressure reducing valve, a closed isolation valve, increased system demand, or even a faulty pressure sensor.

Likewise, increasing pump run times do not automatically mean a pump is failing. Engineers must consider changing demands, force main conditions, wet well behavior, recent maintenance activities, and the overall hydraulic performance of the system.

This is where engineering judgment begins long before predictive models enter the discussion.

Engineers combine operational data with hydraulic principles, system knowledge, design experience, and field observations to determine not only **what** is happening, but **why** it is happening.

Understanding the cause is essential before attempting to predict the future.

## Predictive Analytics: Preparing for Tomorrow

Operational analytics answers an important question:

_**"What is happening?"**_

Engineering analysis explains:

_**"Why is it happening?"**_

But municipalities cannot afford to spend all of their time responding to today's problems.

They must also understand where tomorrow's risks are developing.

This is where predictive analytics becomes valuable.

Historical operational data often contains subtle patterns that develop long before a major failure occurs.

Consider a wastewater lift station. A single increase in pump run time may simply reflect a rainy day. But if pump run times gradually increase over several months while flows remain relatively constant, engineers may begin investigating pump wear, force main fouling, or declining hydraulic efficiency.

Likewise, a water distribution system may experience occasional pressure fluctuations that are perfectly normal. However, if those fluctuations become more frequent within the same pressure zone over time, they may indicate developing valve problems, increasing leakage, or changing system demands.

These gradual changes often go unnoticed during day-to-day operations because each individual event appears insignificant.

Predictive analytics looks for these long-term trends before they develop into service interruptions or costly emergency repairs.

Predictive analytics combines operational data with engineering knowledge, historical performance, statistical analysis, and asset information to estimate where future problems are most likely to develop.

Imagine a utility responsible for several hundred miles of water main. Replacing every pipe before it fails is financially impossible, while waiting for failures can be even more expensive. By combining historical break records, pipe material, installation dates, soil conditions, operating pressures, maintenance history, and operational performance, predictive models can estimate which areas present the highest probability of future failures.

Likewise, two pumps of the same age may have completely different operational histories. One may continue operating efficiently for years, while another may already be showing measurable signs of deterioration. Predictive analytics helps distinguish between those two situations.

Its purpose is not to predict the future with certainty.

Its purpose is to reduce uncertainty so engineers can make better decisions before failures become emergencies.

## Why Municipalities Benefit

Municipal utilities operate under constant constraints.

Budgets are limited.

Staffing is often stretched thin.

Infrastructure continues to age.

Customer expectations continue to rise.

Every maintenance activity, inspection, rehabilitation project, and capital improvement competes for limited resources.

Operational analytics helps municipalities respond effectively when problems occur.

Engineering analysis helps municipalities understand why those problems occurred.

Predictive analytics helps municipalities reduce the number of problems that occur in the first place.

Together, they help municipalities:

- Detect water main breaks and dispatch repair crews more quickly.
- Identify declining pump performance before catastrophic failures occur.
- Prioritize valve exercising and hydrant maintenance using operational history.
- Schedule equipment replacement during planned outages instead of emergency repairs.
- Target pipeline inspections toward areas exhibiting elevated risk.
- Prioritize capital improvement projects using objective operational evidence.
- Reduce emergency callouts, overtime, and unplanned service interruptions.
- Extend the useful life of assets that continue to perform reliably.
- Improve long-term capital planning.
- Make better use of limited financial and personnel resources.

Perhaps most importantly, they allow engineering teams to spend less time reacting to failures and more time preventing them.

Predictive analytics does not eliminate uncertainty.

It helps engineers manage it.

## Better Data. Better Decisions

As utilities continue investing in sensors, digital infrastructure, and data collection, the greatest opportunity isn't collecting more information.

It is transforming information into engineering insight.

Operational analytics provides awareness.

Engineering analysis provides understanding.

Predictive analytics provides foresight.

Engineering judgment turns all three into action.

That is the engineering decision lifecycle.

Data becomes information.

Information becomes insight.

Insight supports better engineering decisions.

And better engineering decisions create more reliable, resilient, and cost-effective infrastructure for the communities we serve.
