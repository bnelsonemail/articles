# The Cost of Staying Deep

During design of a large-diameter force main, the pipeline profile was forced beneath several existing utilities before returning to grade.

Individually, each crossing wasn't a problem.

Collectively, they created a different question.

> Do we keep the pipeline deep to eliminate the high points, or return to grade and accept additional air release valves?

From a hydraulic standpoint, either option could work.

The harder question was economic.

---

## The Hidden Cost of Going Deeper

Keeping a pipeline deeper isn't simply a matter of digging a little farther.

As depth increases, excavation quantities grow, dewatering becomes more challenging, maintenance of traffic costs increase, and future repairs become more complicated.

Depending on the project, just a few additional feet of depth over an extended distance can significantly affect construction costs.

---

## The Cost of Additional Air Release Valves

Returning to grade isn't free either.

Each additional air release valve adds construction cost, ongoing maintenance, future replacement, and another asset the utility must manage throughout the life of the system.

For wastewater force mains, localized high points can also introduce operational concerns such as air accumulation and hydrogen sulfide buildup, increasing the importance of proper valve placement and long-term maintenance.

---

## There Isn't One Right Answer

Some projects may justify staying deep for a few hundred feet to eliminate an air release valve.

On others, the additional excavation, dewatering, and traffic control quickly outweigh the long-term cost of another valve.

That raises an interesting question:

> At what point does staying deep become more expensive than installing another air release valve?

The answer depends on many factors, including:

- Groundwater conditions
- Construction costs
- Maintenance of Traffic (MOT) requirements
- Future maintenance and repair costs
- Utility operational capabilities
- Long-term lifecycle considerations

There isn't a universal break-even point.

But there *is* a structured way to evaluate the tradeoff.
