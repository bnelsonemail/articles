# Engineering Judgment Begins Where the Calculations End

One of the biggest lessons I've learned over the years is that the most important engineering decisions often aren't the technical ones.

They're the economic ones.

As engineers, we're trained to answer questions like:

* Will it work?
* Does it meet code?
* Is it hydraulically acceptable?
* Is it structurally adequate?

Those questions are essential.

But they aren't always the questions that determine the best solution.

With experience, the questions begin to change.

Instead of asking only whether something can be built, you begin asking:

* How difficult will it be to maintain?
* What happens when it fails twenty years from now?
* Can the owner realistically maintain this asset?
* Does reducing today's construction cost increase tomorrow's maintenance cost?
* Are we optimizing for the contractor...or for the owner?

Those questions rarely have a single correct answer.

They require engineering judgment.

I've found that some of the best engineering discussions I've been part of weren't about calculations at all. They were about balancing construction cost, operations, maintenance, risk, and long-term ownership.

That's one of the reasons I enjoy municipal infrastructure.

Our projects are expected to serve communities for decades. Every design decision becomes someone else's responsibility to operate, maintain, repair, and eventually replace.

The drawing set may be complete when construction ends.

The engineering decision isn't.

Experience hasn't simply given me more answers.

It's changed the questions I ask.

I'd be interested to hear what questions experience has taught other engineers to ask before making a design decision.
