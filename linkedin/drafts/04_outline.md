# The Cost of Staying Deep: Evaluating Force Main Profile Tradeoffs

## Introduction

In a previous article, I discussed a force main design where utility crossings created several localized high points along the pipeline profile. The design team faced a common question:

> Should the force main remain deeper for an extended distance to avoid high points, or should the profile return to grade and rely on additional air release valves (ARVs)?

From a hydraulic perspective, either solution could work.

The more difficult question was economic.

This article examines the cost considerations associated with each approach and explores where the break-even point may occur between remaining deep and accepting additional high points.

---

# Assumptions and Limitations

## Project Assumptions

For purposes of discussion, the following assumptions are used:

* 12-inch PVC force main
* Urban Florida environment
* Coastal groundwater conditions
* Open-cut construction
* Moderate to heavy traffic impacts requiring MOT
* Multiple utility crossings creating localized profile changes

## Important Note

The objective is not to develop a universal answer.

The break-even point will vary based on:

* Pipe diameter
* Pipe material
* Groundwater conditions
* Traffic conditions
* Utility congestion
* Local construction costs
* Utility operational preferences
* Utility maintenance capabilities

This article focuses on illustrating the decision-making process rather than providing a single design standard.

---

# The Two Competing Approaches

## Option 1: Stay Deep

The force main remains at a deeper elevation through and beyond utility crossings.

### Advantages

* Fewer localized high points
* Fewer ARVs required
* Reduced potential for air accumulation
* Reduced H₂S accumulation concerns
* Fewer assets requiring long-term maintenance
* Reduced number of above-grade appurtenances

### Disadvantages

* Increased excavation costs
* Increased dewatering costs
* Increased MOT duration
* Increased construction risk
* Potential utility conflict escalation
* Increased future repair complexity

---

## Option 2: Return to Grade

The force main returns to a shallower profile after each utility crossing.

### Advantages

* Reduced excavation volume
* Reduced dewatering requirements
* Faster construction
* Lower risk during installation
* Easier future access for repairs

### Disadvantages

* Additional ARVs required
* Increased O&M burden
* Increased lifecycle replacement costs
* Potential odor and corrosion concerns
* Additional appurtenances requiring access

---

# Understanding the Cost of Staying Deep

## Excavation Costs

As pipeline depth increases:

* Excavation volume increases
* Haul-off quantities increase
* Backfill quantities increase
* Trench safety requirements become more significant

The increase is not linear.

A few additional feet of depth can substantially increase total excavation costs.

---

## Dewatering Costs

In coastal Florida, dewatering often becomes one of the most significant cost drivers.

### Factors Affecting Dewatering Cost

* Groundwater elevation
* Soil conditions
* Permit requirements
* Treatment requirements
* Duration of pumping

For some projects, maintaining an additional few feet of depth over a long distance may require continuous dewatering operations.

The resulting costs can quickly exceed the cost of additional ARVs.

---

## Maintenance of Traffic (MOT)

Urban environments introduce another major consideration.

Deeper excavations frequently result in:

* Longer construction durations
* Larger work zones
* Additional lane closure durations
* Greater traffic disruption

As depth increases, MOT costs often increase alongside excavation and dewatering costs.

---

## Construction Risk

Deep excavations generally involve:

* Greater worker safety concerns
* Increased utility conflict exposure
* Higher risk of unstable trench conditions
* Reduced contractor productivity

These risks may not always be directly reflected in bid prices but frequently influence overall project cost.

---

# Understanding the Cost of Additional ARVs

## Initial Construction Costs

Each additional ARV introduces:

* Valve cost
* Vault cost
* Piping and fittings
* Access improvements
* Installation labor

The cumulative cost can become significant when multiple high points are present.

---

## Operations and Maintenance Costs

Unlike buried pipe, ARVs require periodic attention.

Potential activities include:

* Inspection
* Cleaning
* Testing
* Repair
* Replacement of internal components

Over time, these costs accumulate.

---

## Asset Replacement Costs

A force main may remain in service for decades.

ARVs typically have shorter service lives.

Future costs may include:

* Valve replacement
* Vault rehabilitation
* Access improvements
* Traffic control during maintenance

These lifecycle costs should be considered alongside initial construction costs.

---

## Operational Risks

For wastewater force mains, high points can create additional concerns:

* Air accumulation
* Reduced hydraulic efficiency
* Hydrogen sulfide accumulation
* Odor generation
* Corrosion potential

These risks are difficult to quantify but should be considered during design.

---

# Long-Term Maintenance and Emergency Repair Considerations

Construction cost is only one component of lifecycle cost.

A force main may remain in service for 50 to 100 years, during which time repairs, modifications, and emergency maintenance may be required.

The depth selected during design can significantly influence future repair costs.

---

## Deep Pipe Considerations

A deeper force main may require:

* Larger excavation areas
* Additional shoring requirements
* More extensive dewatering
* Longer repair durations
* Greater traffic impacts
* Increased contractor mobilization costs

In some cases, municipalities may not have the equipment or personnel necessary to safely perform deep excavations.

Emergency repairs may require:

* Specialty excavation contractors
* Emergency dewatering operations
* Additional traffic control measures
* Extended service outages

As a result, emergency repairs can become substantially more expensive than similar repairs performed on shallower infrastructure.

---

## Shallow Pipe Considerations

A shallower force main generally allows:

* Faster access during emergencies
* Reduced excavation quantities
* Simpler dewatering requirements
* Reduced traffic disruption
* Greater likelihood that utility crews can perform repairs in-house

These benefits may offset some of the additional lifecycle costs associated with ARVs.

---

# Utility Owner Perspective

One factor often overlooked during design is the utility's ability to maintain the infrastructure after construction is complete.

Not all utilities have the same staffing levels, equipment inventories, or operational capabilities.

---

## Utility A: Large Utility

Characteristics:

* Dedicated maintenance crews
* Excavation equipment
* Specialized utility staff
* Established ARV maintenance programs

Potential Preference:

* Shallower force main
* Additional ARVs
* Reduced future repair complexity

---

## Utility B: Smaller Utility

Characteristics:

* Limited maintenance personnel
* Limited equipment resources
* Greater reliance on outside contractors

Potential Preference:

* Fewer ARVs
* Simpler system configuration
* Reduced appurtenance maintenance

---

## Why This Matters

The lowest construction cost is not always the lowest ownership cost.

The preferred solution may depend as much on utility operations as on engineering economics.

---

# Developing a Break-Even Evaluation

## The Core Question

The design decision can be simplified to:

> How much additional deep pipe can be constructed before the cost exceeds the cost of installing, maintaining, and eventually replacing another ARV?

This becomes the break-even point.

---

## Variables Affecting the Break-Even Point

### Variables Favoring Staying Deep

* High ARV costs
* Difficult maintenance access
* Significant odor concerns
* Utility preference for fewer appurtenances
* Limited maintenance staff

### Variables Favoring Returning to Grade

* High groundwater levels
* Expensive dewatering
* Significant MOT requirements
* Long lengths of additional deep pipe
* High future repair costs associated with deep excavations

---

# Example Evaluation Framework

## Additional Cost of Staying Deep

### Construction Cost

Consider:

* Additional excavation
* Additional dewatering
* Additional MOT
* Additional construction duration

Construction Cost = Excavation + Dewatering + MOT + Construction Impacts

---

### Future Maintenance and Repair Cost

Consider:

* Increased repair complexity
* Increased dewatering during repairs
* Increased traffic control during repairs
* Contractor mobilization requirements

Future Repair Cost = Expected Repair Events × Additional Cost Due to Depth

---

### Total Deep Profile Lifecycle Cost

Deep Profile Lifecycle Cost = Construction Cost + Future Repair Cost

---

## Additional Cost of Returning to Grade

### Initial Construction Cost

Consider:

* ARV installation
* Vault construction
* Piping modifications
* Site access improvements

---

### Lifecycle ARV Cost

Consider:

* Inspection
* Maintenance
* Component replacement
* Valve replacement
* Future traffic control during maintenance

Lifecycle ARV Cost = Installation + O&M + Replacement

---

## Finding the Crossover Point

The decision point occurs where:

Deep Profile Lifecycle Cost = ARV Lifecycle Cost

Beyond that point, returning to grade may become the more economical option.

Before that point, remaining deep may provide better value.

---

# Conceptual Example

The following example is intended only to illustrate the evaluation process. Actual costs will vary significantly based on location, pipe size, groundwater conditions, traffic impacts, and utility requirements.

## Assumed Scenario

A 12-inch PVC force main encounters a utility conflict that creates a localized high point.

The design team has two options:

### Option 1: Stay Deep

Remain approximately 4 feet deeper than the preferred profile for an extended distance.

### Option 2: Return to Grade

Return to the preferred profile and install an additional air release valve at the resulting high point.

---

### Additional Deep Pipe Costs

| Additional dewatering           | $X/LF        |
| Additional MOT                  | $X/LF        |
| Additional construction impacts | $X/LF        |

Total Additional Deep Pipe Cost:

Deep Pipe Cost = $Y/LF

---

### Additional ARV Costs

| Cost Component               | Assumed Cost |
| ---------------------------- | ------------ |
| ARV assembly                 | $X           |
| Vault and appurtenances      | $X           |
| Installation                 | $X           |
| Future maintenance allowance | $X           |
| Future replacement allowance | $X           |

Total Lifecycle ARV Cost:

ARV Lifecycle Cost = $Z

---

## Break-Even Evaluation

The break-even distance can be estimated as:

Break-Even Length = ARV Lifecycle Cost ÷ Deep Pipe Cost per Linear Foot

Example:

Break-Even Length = $25,000 ÷ $50/LF

Break-Even Length = 500 LF

Under this simplified example:

* If avoiding the ARV requires less than 500 LF of additional deep pipe, remaining deep may be the lower-cost alternative.
* If avoiding the ARV requires more than 500 LF of additional deep pipe, installing the ARV may become the lower-cost alternative.

---

## Additional Considerations

This simplified example does not account for:

* Utility maintenance preferences
* Future emergency repair costs
* Odor concerns
* Hydrogen sulfide accumulation
* Reliability considerations
* Access limitations
* Utility staffing and equipment availability

As a result, the lowest-cost alternative may not always be the preferred alternative.

# Inputs Required for a Project-Specific Analysis

The conceptual example presented above illustrates the evaluation process, but actual projects require a more detailed assessment.

The break-even point between remaining deep and installing additional air release valves is highly sensitive to project-specific conditions. Developing a meaningful analysis requires identifying the major variables that influence both construction and lifecycle costs.

## Force Main Characteristics

The physical characteristics of the force main directly influence both construction and maintenance costs.

Examples include:

* Pipe diameter
* Pipe material
* Design flow conditions
* Required depth of cover
* Additional depth required to avoid high points
* Additional length of deep installation

Larger diameter pipelines generally result in wider excavations, larger trench support systems, increased dewatering requirements, and higher future repair costs.

This article uses a 12-inch PVC force main as a representative example. However, the economics may change substantially for larger diameter force mains. Larger pipes generally require wider excavations, larger trench support systems, more extensive dewatering operations, and greater future repair costs. As a result, the break-even point may shift considerably as pipe diameter increases.

---

## Site Conditions

Site-specific conditions often have a significant impact on the economics of deep construction.

Examples include:

* Groundwater elevation
* Soil conditions
* Existing utility congestion
* Available right-of-way
* Environmental permitting requirements
* Proximity to sensitive infrastructure

In coastal Florida, groundwater conditions alone can significantly alter the economics of remaining deep.

---

## Construction Cost Variables

Several construction-related factors should be considered when evaluating additional depth.

Examples include:

* Excavation costs
* Backfill costs
* Dewatering costs
* Trench safety requirements
* Maintenance of Traffic (MOT)
* Construction duration impacts

These costs often increase as depth increases and may become major drivers of the overall decision.

---

## Air Release Valve Cost Variables

The lifecycle cost of ARVs extends beyond initial installation.

Examples include:

* Valve procurement costs
* Vault construction costs
* Installation costs
* Access improvements
* Inspection and maintenance requirements
* Future replacement costs

These costs should be evaluated over the anticipated service life of the force main.

---

## Utility Operational Considerations

Not all utilities operate and maintain infrastructure in the same manner.

Examples include:

* Available maintenance staff
* Available excavation equipment
* Contractor reliance
* Maintenance philosophy
* Emergency response capabilities

A utility with dedicated maintenance crews may evaluate the decision differently than a utility that relies heavily on outside contractors.

---

## Lifecycle and Risk Considerations

Some of the most important inputs are also the most difficult to quantify.

Examples include:

* Expected service life
* Emergency repair frequency
* Additional repair costs associated with depth
* Odor concerns
* Hydrogen sulfide accumulation potential
* Reliability requirements
* Utility risk tolerance

Although these factors may not appear directly in a bid tabulation, they can significantly influence the total cost of ownership over the life of the asset.

---

## From Engineering Judgment to Decision Support

The purpose of identifying these inputs is not to eliminate engineering judgment.

Rather, it is to create a structured framework for evaluating alternatives.

As more project-specific information becomes available, these variables can be incorporated into increasingly detailed analyses ranging from simple break-even calculations to lifecycle cost evaluations and risk-based decision models.

The goal is not simply to determine whether a force main should remain deep or return to grade.

The goal is to understand the long-term consequences of each option and make a decision that aligns with the utility's operational, financial, and maintenance objectives.

# Why the Answer Is Rarely Simple

The break-even point is influenced by many factors beyond construction cost.

Examples include:

* Utility staffing levels
* Equipment availability
* Maintenance philosophy
* Future accessibility
* Reliability requirements
* Risk tolerance
* Local contractor market conditions

As a result, two experienced engineers may reasonably arrive at different conclusions while evaluating the same alignment.

That does not necessarily mean either solution is wrong.

It simply reflects different assumptions regarding lifecycle cost and operational priorities.

---

# The Bigger Lesson

Many infrastructure design decisions are not purely hydraulic decisions.

They are lifecycle cost decisions.

The most economical solution is not always the shallowest profile or the deepest profile.

The most economical solution is often the one that balances:

* Construction cost
* Operational cost
* Maintenance burden
* Asset longevity
* Repairability
* Utility capabilities
* Risk tolerance

Understanding where those costs intersect is often more valuable than focusing on any single design variable.

For force mains, the real question is rarely whether a high point can be avoided.

The more important question is whether avoiding it creates enough value to justify the cost.

The break-even point is not necessarily where the decision becomes obvious. It is simply where the economics begin to favor one option over the other.
