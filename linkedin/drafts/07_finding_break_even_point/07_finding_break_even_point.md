# Finding the Break-Even Point: A Lifecycle Framework for Evaluating Force Main Profile Tradeoffs

In Part 1 of this series, I discussed why the decision to remain deep or install additional air release valves extends well beyond hydraulic calculations.

While both approaches may satisfy the hydraulic requirements of the system, they often differ significantly in construction cost, maintenance requirements, operational complexity, and long-term ownership costs.

That naturally leads to the next question:

> **Can these competing costs be evaluated in a structured way?**

I believe they can.

The objective isn't to develop a universal equation that tells us which design is "correct." Every force main project is different.

Instead, the goal is to create a framework that helps engineers evaluate the economic consequences of each alternative in a consistent way.

Every project brings its own variables. Pipe diameter, groundwater conditions, soil characteristics, utility congestion, maintenance of traffic requirements, local construction costs, and even the owner's maintenance philosophy all influence the decision.

That's why there isn't a universal break-even point.

There is, however, a universal process for thinking through the problem.

The first step is understanding the additional cost of remaining deep.

Those costs extend far beyond simply excavating a deeper trench. Depending on the project, additional depth may increase excavation quantities, trench safety requirements, dewatering, construction duration, utility conflicts, and contractor productivity impacts. In many coastal environments, dewatering alone can become a significant contributor to construction cost.

Returning the pipeline to a shallower profile often reduces many of those construction costs, but it introduces a different set of long-term obligations.

An additional air release valve isn't simply the cost of purchasing a valve. It represents decades of inspections, preventative maintenance, component replacement, traffic control, and eventual replacement. Those activities become part of the asset's lifecycle cost.

Another consideration that is easy to overlook is future repair cost.

Construction happens once.

Maintenance continues for the life of the infrastructure.

A deeper force main may require larger excavations, additional shoring, more extensive dewatering, longer repair durations, and greater traffic impacts. A shallower installation may allow future repairs to be completed more efficiently, but only after accepting the long-term maintenance responsibilities associated with additional appurtenances.

The owner's capabilities also matter.

A large utility with dedicated maintenance crews, specialized equipment, and established valve maintenance programs may view these tradeoffs very differently than a smaller utility that relies heavily on outside contractors.

Neither approach is inherently better.

The best solution is often the one that best aligns with the owner's long-term operational capabilities.

Conceptually, the comparison is straightforward.

### Deep Profile Lifecycle Cost

- Construction
- Expected Future Repairs

### Air Release Valve Lifecycle Cost

- Installation
- Maintenance
- Replacement

At some point, those two lifecycle costs become approximately equal.

Beyond that point, one alternative begins to provide greater long-term value than the other.

Where that crossover occurs will vary from project to project.

To illustrate the concept, consider a simplified example.

Suppose maintaining a deeper profile adds approximately **$50 per linear foot** in construction cost, while the lifecycle cost of installing and maintaining an additional air release valve is estimated at **$25,000**.

Under those assumptions, the conceptual break-even point would occur at roughly **500 feet** of additional deep construction.

That doesn't mean every project should use 500 feet as a design threshold.

Far from it.

The value of the exercise isn't the number.

It's understanding **how** the number is developed.

Even then, not every consideration fits neatly into a spreadsheet.

Hydrogen sulfide accumulation, odor concerns, staffing levels, emergency response capabilities, public disruption, and owner risk tolerance all influence the final decision.

Engineering judgment remains essential.

A lifecycle framework doesn't replace that judgment.

It simply provides better information upon which to base it.

For me, that's one of the most interesting aspects of infrastructure design.

There is rarely a single "correct" answer.

Our role isn't simply to make infrastructure work.

It's to understand the long-term consequences of the decisions we make and select the solution that provides the greatest overall value to the owner.

That's where engineering judgment and lifecycle thinking come together.
