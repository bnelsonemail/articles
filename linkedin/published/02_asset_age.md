# Why Asset Age Is a Poor Predictor of Infrastructure Failure

When discussing infrastructure replacement priorities, one of the first questions often asked is:

"How old is the pipe?"

It's a reasonable question, but in my experience it is rarely the most important one.

Over the years, I've seen relatively young assets experience significant deterioration while much older infrastructure continued to perform reliably. Age certainly matters, but it is only one piece of a much larger story.

Consider a wastewater force main constructed of ductile iron pipe. Many engineers would expect the material to provide decades of service. Yet operating conditions can dramatically influence actual asset life. In some systems, hydrogen sulfide generation and subsequent corrosion can create severe internal deterioration long before the asset reaches what might traditionally be considered the end of its useful life.

From an asset management perspective, the pipe's age alone tells us very little about its true condition.

The more important questions are:

- What environment is the asset operating in?
- What loading conditions has it experienced?
- How has it been maintained?
- What is its failure history?
- What are the consequences if it fails?

These factors often provide a much clearer picture of risk than age by itself.

This is one reason why utilities are increasingly moving toward risk-based asset management approaches. Rather than relying solely on installation dates, they are incorporating condition assessments, operational history, environmental factors, and consequence-of-failure evaluations into their decision-making process.

Infrastructure does not age uniformly.

Two assets installed on the same day can have vastly different remaining service lives depending on how and where they operate.

Age remains an important data point. It simply should not be mistaken for the whole story.

The challenge for utilities is not identifying the oldest assets.

The challenge is identifying the assets most likely to fail before they do.
