# Beyond Engineering Judgment: How Infrastructure Data Could Transform Lifecycle Decision Making

## Introduction

In the first two articles of this series, I explored a relatively straightforward engineering question:

> Should a force main remain deeper to eliminate localized high points, or should it return to a shallower profile and rely on additional air release valves?

From a hydraulic perspective, either solution could satisfy the design requirements.

The more challenging question involved balancing construction cost, long-term maintenance, operational complexity, and lifecycle economics. Like many engineering decisions, there wasn't a single "correct" answer. The appropriate solution depended on the specific circumstances of the project and the experience of the engineering team.

That naturally raises another question.

**What if engineers had access to decades of historical data that could help inform those decisions?**

Not to replace engineering judgment, but to strengthen it.

---

## Engineering Judgment Has Always Been Data Driven

Engineering has never been about calculations alone.

Every experienced engineer carries a mental library of past projects, construction challenges, maintenance issues, conversations with operators, lessons learned in the field, and countless decisions that succeeded or failed over the course of a career.

Those experiences become part of engineering judgment.

When an engineer recommends one design over another, that recommendation is often influenced by much more than equations. It reflects years of accumulated observations, practical experience, and an understanding of how infrastructure performs long after construction is complete.

The challenge is that much of this knowledge exists only in people's memories or is scattered across reports, spreadsheets, maintenance records, and filing cabinets.

As experienced professionals retire, much of that institutional knowledge retires with them.

---

## Utilities Are Collecting More Information Than Ever

Over the past two decades, utilities have quietly accumulated enormous amounts of operational data.

Many organizations now maintain information such as:

- Asset inventories
- Construction and replacement costs
- Repair histories
- Preventive maintenance records
- CMMS work orders
- SCADA operating data
- Pump runtimes
- Pressure and flow measurements
- Customer service requests
- GIS information
- Inspection reports

Most of these systems were implemented to help utilities operate more efficiently, manage assets, or satisfy regulatory requirements.

They were not originally designed to support engineering design decisions.

Yet together they represent something incredibly valuable:

**A historical record of how infrastructure actually performs.**

---

## The Opportunity Isn't More Data

When discussions turn toward artificial intelligence, the conversation often begins with collecting more data.

In many cases, utilities already have plenty of information.

The larger challenge is connecting those independent datasets in ways that support better engineering decisions.

Construction costs may exist in one database.

Maintenance history may exist in another.

SCADA information may be stored elsewhere.

Inspection reports may still be PDFs.

Individually, each dataset tells part of the story.

Together, they begin to describe the lifecycle of an asset.

That shift, from isolated information to connected knowledge, may ultimately prove more valuable than simply collecting additional data.

---

## Reimagining the Force Main Decision

Consider the force main example from the previous articles.

Today, an engineer evaluating the deep versus shallow alternatives might rely on design standards, cost estimates, engineering judgment, and discussions with utility staff.

Now imagine supplementing that process with historical data from hundreds of similar projects.

Instead of estimating maintenance requirements, the engineer could review actual maintenance histories for comparable air release valve installations.

Instead of assuming excavation costs, historical bid data could reveal how construction costs changed under similar groundwater conditions.

Instead of relying solely on experience, engineers could examine records showing how frequently certain configurations required repairs, generated odor complaints, or resulted in emergency maintenance.

The engineer still makes the decision.

The difference is that the decision is informed by far more evidence than was previously available.

---

## From Deterministic Design to Probabilistic Thinking

Engineering calculations often produce a single answer.

Reality rarely does.

Construction costs vary.

Equipment ages differently.

Ground conditions change.

Infrastructure rarely behaves exactly as predicted.

As historical data becomes more accessible, engineers may increasingly evaluate decisions in terms of probabilities rather than certainties.

Instead of asking:

> Which alternative costs less?

Future analyses may ask:

> Which alternative is more likely to produce the lowest total lifecycle cost over the next thirty years?

Rather than predicting one outcome, engineers can begin evaluating a range of possible outcomes and the likelihood of each occurring.

That shift better reflects the uncertainty that has always existed in engineering practice.

---

## Engineering Judgment Remains Essential

None of this suggests that engineering judgment becomes less important.

Quite the opposite.

Every utility has different priorities.

Budgets differ.

Risk tolerance differs.

Operations differ.

Political considerations differ.

Community expectations differ.

Data can identify patterns, quantify uncertainty, and reveal trends that might otherwise go unnoticed.

It cannot determine what level of risk is acceptable, how projects should be prioritized, or which tradeoffs best serve a particular community.

Those decisions will continue to require experienced engineers who understand both the numbers and the broader context in which infrastructure operates.

---

## Looking Ahead

I believe this is one of the most exciting directions for the civil engineering profession.

Not because engineering judgment is being replaced.

Because it is being strengthened.

As utilities continue improving asset management systems, collecting operational data, and digitizing decades of historical records, new opportunities will emerge to support better lifecycle planning through statistical analysis, predictive modeling, and decision-support tools.

These technologies are not substitutes for sound engineering.

They are additional tools that can help engineers better understand uncertainty, evaluate alternatives, and make more informed decisions.

---

## From Data to Decision Support

This evolution is one of the reasons I've become increasingly interested in engineering software and infrastructure analytics.

Over the past few years, I've been exploring how modern software development, statistical analysis, and machine learning can complement traditional engineering practice. Not by replacing engineering judgment, but by helping engineers organize information, quantify uncertainty, and evaluate alternatives more effectively.

Some of that work has focused on building hydraulic analysis software. Other projects explore infrastructure anomaly detection, asset analytics, and ways of transforming operational data into meaningful engineering insights.

While these efforts are still evolving, they all share the same objective:

**Helping engineers make better decisions with better information.**

Whether the question involves hydraulic design, asset management, lifecycle cost, or infrastructure reliability, the goal remains the same. Engineering software should support sound engineering judgment, not replace it.

I believe that represents one of the most exciting opportunities for our profession over the coming decades.

---

## Conclusion

This three-part series began with a simple question about force main profile design.

Along the way, it expanded into discussions about lifecycle economics, uncertainty, and the role of engineering judgment in complex infrastructure decisions.

For me, that's one of the most rewarding aspects of engineering.

The calculations are important, but they are rarely the end of the story.

The real challenge lies in weighing competing priorities, understanding uncertainty, and making decisions that will continue serving communities decades after construction is complete.

As better data becomes available, I believe engineers will have an opportunity to make those decisions with greater confidence than ever before.

Not because the profession is changing its purpose.

Because it is gaining better information to fulfill it.
