# When Utility Crossings Create High Points: A Force Main Design Decision

## Introduction

* Brief overview of the problem.

  * Design decisions regarding force main profiles due to utility crossings.
  * Utility crossings often force pipelines to rise and fall, creating localized high points.
  * These high points can introduce operational and maintenance challenges that must be considered during design.

### Why Are Air Release Valves (ARVs) Necessary?

* Air release valves are commonly installed at high points in a pipeline.
* Air naturally migrates to and accumulates at high points within the system.
* Without ARVs, trapped air can:

  * Restrict flow.
  * Increase headloss.
  * Reduce hydraulic efficiency.
  * Negatively impact pump performance.
  * Contribute to transient pressure issues.

### Additional Considerations for Wastewater Force Mains

* In wastewater force mains, gases accumulating at high points may contain hydrogen sulfide (H₂S).
* Hydrogen sulfide is generated within the wastewater system under septic conditions.
* Accumulated H₂S can:

  * Accelerate internal corrosion.
  * Damage pipeline components and appurtenances.
  * Contribute to odor complaints.
  * Reduce the overall service life of the asset.
* Proper gas management is therefore important for both hydraulic performance and long-term asset preservation.

### Central Design Question

* When utility crossings create high points, should the profile be lowered to minimize high points, or should additional ARVs be installed?
* The answer is often project-specific and requires balancing construction costs, operational considerations, and long-term maintenance requirements.

---

## Why High Points Matter

* Air and gases naturally accumulate at high points within the force main.
* Trapped air can reduce the effective cross-sectional area of the pipe.
* Gas accumulation may:

  * Increase headloss.
  * Reduce system efficiency.
  * Affect pump operation.
  * Create maintenance concerns.
* In wastewater systems, hydrogen sulfide accumulation may increase corrosion risk and shorten pipeline life.

---

## Design Considerations and Trade-Offs

### Option 1: Deeper Profile with Fewer High Points

#### Advantages

* Fewer high points within the system.
* Reduced number of ARVs.
* Fewer appurtenances requiring maintenance.
* Reduced potential for odor release locations.
* Simpler long-term operation.

#### Disadvantages

* Increased excavation depth.
* Greater shoring requirements.
* Increased dewatering requirements.
* Higher construction costs.
* More difficult future repairs and maintenance activities.
* Increased construction risk in areas with poor soils or high groundwater.

---

### Option 2: Shallower Profile with Additional ARVs

#### Advantages

* Reduced excavation depth.
* Reduced shoring requirements.
* Potentially lower construction costs.
* Easier access to the pipeline for future repairs.
* Reduced impacts associated with deep installations.

#### Disadvantages

* Additional ARVs requiring inspection and maintenance.
* Increased lifecycle costs.
* More potential points of failure.
* Potential odor concerns.
* Traffic control requirements during future maintenance activities.
* Additional operational responsibilities for utility staff.

---

## Additional Factors Influencing the Decision

### Construction Environment

* Urban environments may increase costs due to:

  * Traffic control requirements.
  * Limited working space.
  * Utility congestion.
  * Impacts to businesses and residents.
* Deep profiles may become significantly more expensive under these conditions.

### Groundwater Conditions

* Deep excavations may require substantial dewatering.
* High groundwater levels can significantly increase construction costs and risks.

### Utility Owner Preferences

* Some owners prefer minimizing ARVs to reduce long-term maintenance obligations.
* Others may prioritize lower initial construction costs and accept additional maintenance requirements.

### Maintenance Team Considerations

* Availability of maintenance staff.
* Access to specialized equipment.
* Ability to routinely inspect and maintain ARVs.
* Long-term operational capabilities of the utility.

### Accessibility

* Deep pipelines can be difficult and expensive to repair.
* ARVs may provide easier access points but require periodic maintenance.
* Accessibility should be considered over the entire life of the asset.

### Easement and Right-of-Way Constraints

* Available easement width.
* Existing utility conflicts.
* Future development considerations.
* Access for maintenance vehicles and personnel.

---

## The Real Trade-Off

* Initial construction cost versus long-term maintenance cost.
* Construction complexity versus operational complexity.
* Reduced excavation versus increased appurtenances.
* Capital expenditures versus lifecycle costs.
* Contractor convenience versus utility maintenance burden.

---

## Conclusion

* There is rarely a single correct answer when utility crossings create high points in a force main.
* The optimal solution depends on:

  * Site conditions.
  * Utility owner preferences.
  * Construction constraints.
  * Maintenance capabilities.
  * Long-term operational goals.
* Successful force main design requires balancing construction cost, maintainability, reliability, accessibility, and lifecycle performance.
* Engineering judgment is often required to determine which trade-offs provide the greatest overall value throughout the life of the system.
* Ultimately, the goal is to design a system that meets hydraulic requirements while minimizing long-term risks and costs associated with maintenance and operation.
