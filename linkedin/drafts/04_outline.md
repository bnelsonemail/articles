# The Cost of Staying Deep: Evaluating Force Main Profile Tradeoffs

## Introduction

In a previous article, I discussed a force main design where utility crossings created several localized high points along the pipeline profile. The design team faced a common question:

> Should the force main remain deeper for an extended distance to avoid high points, or should the profile return to grade and rely on additional air release valves (ARVs)?

From a hydraulic perspective, either solution could work.

The more difficult question was economic.

This article examines the cost considerations associated with each approach and explores where the break-even point may occur between remaining deep and accepting additional high points.

---

## Assumptions and Limitations

### Project Assumptions

For purposes of discussion, the following assumptions are used:

* 12-inch PVC force main
* Urban Florida environment
* Coastal groundwater conditions
* Open-cut construction
* Moderate to heavy traffic impacts requiring MOT
* Multiple utility crossings creating localized profile changes

### Important Note

The objective is not to develop a universal answer.

The break-even point will vary based on:

* Pipe diameter
* Pipe material
* Groundwater conditions
* Traffic conditions
* Utility congestion
* Local construction costs
* Utility operational preferences

This article focuses on illustrating the decision-making process rather than providing a single design standard.

---

# The Two Competing Approaches

## Option 1: Stay Deep

The force main remains at a deeper elevation through and beyond utility crossings.

### Advantages

* Fewer localized high points
* Fewer ARVs required
* Reduced potential for air accumulation
* Reduced H₂S accumulation concerns
* Fewer assets requiring long-term maintenance

### Disadvantages

* Increased excavation costs
* Increased dewatering costs
* Increased MOT duration
* Increased construction risk
* Potential utility conflict escalation

---

## Option 2: Return to Grade

The force main returns to a shallower profile after each utility crossing.

### Advantages

* Reduced excavation volume
* Reduced dewatering requirements
* Faster construction
* Lower risk during installation

### Disadvantages

* Additional ARVs required
* Increased O&M burden
* Increased lifecycle replacement costs
* Potential odor and corrosion concerns
* Additional appurtenances requiring access

---

# Understanding the Cost of Staying Deep

## Excavation Costs

As pipeline depth increases:

* Excavation volume increases
* Haul-off quantities increase
* Backfill quantities increase
* Trench safety requirements become more significant

The increase is not linear.

A few additional feet of depth can substantially increase total excavation costs.

---

## Dewatering Costs

In coastal Florida, dewatering often becomes one of the most significant cost drivers.

### Factors Affecting Dewatering Cost

* Groundwater elevation
* Soil conditions
* Permit requirements
* Treatment requirements
* Duration of pumping

For some projects, maintaining an additional few feet of depth over a long distance may require continuous dewatering operations.

The resulting costs can quickly exceed the cost of additional ARVs.

---

## Maintenance of Traffic (MOT)

Urban environments introduce another major consideration.

Deeper excavations frequently result in:

* Longer construction durations
* Larger work zones
* Additional lane closure durations
* Greater traffic disruption

As depth increases, MOT costs often increase alongside excavation and dewatering costs.

---

## Construction Risk

Deep excavations generally involve:

* Greater worker safety concerns
* Increased utility conflict exposure
* Higher risk of unstable trench conditions
* Reduced contractor productivity

These risks may not always be directly reflected in bid prices but frequently influence overall project cost.

---

# Understanding the Cost of Additional ARVs

## Initial Construction Costs

Each additional ARV introduces:

* Valve cost
* Vault cost
* Piping and fittings
* Access improvements
* Installation labor

The cumulative cost can become significant when multiple high points are present.

---

## Operations and Maintenance Costs

Unlike buried pipe, ARVs require periodic attention.

Potential activities include:

* Inspection
* Cleaning
* Testing
* Repair
* Replacement of internal components

Over time, these costs accumulate.

---

## Asset Replacement Costs

A force main may remain in service for decades.

ARVs typically have shorter service lives.

Future costs may include:

* Valve replacement
* Vault rehabilitation
* Access improvements
* Traffic control during maintenance

These lifecycle costs should be considered alongside initial construction costs.

---

## Operational Risks

For wastewater force mains, high points can create additional concerns:

* Air accumulation
* Reduced hydraulic efficiency
* Hydrogen sulfide accumulation
* Odor generation
* Corrosion potential

These risks are difficult to quantify but should be considered during design.

---

# Developing a Break-Even Evaluation

## The Core Question

The design decision can be simplified to:

> How much additional deep pipe can be constructed before the cost exceeds the cost of installing and maintaining another ARV?

This becomes the break-even point.

---

## Variables Affecting the Break-Even Point

### Variables Favoring Staying Deep

* High ARV costs
* Difficult maintenance access
* Significant odor concerns
* Utility preference for fewer appurtenances

### Variables Favoring Returning to Grade

* High groundwater levels
* Expensive dewatering
* Significant MOT requirements
* Long lengths of additional deep pipe

---

# Example Evaluation Framework

## Additional Cost of Staying Deep

Consider:

* Additional excavation
* Additional dewatering
* Additional MOT
* Additional construction duration

Total Incremental Deep Profile Cost:

Deep Profile Cost = Excavation + Dewatering + MOT + Construction Impacts

---

## Additional Cost of Returning to Grade

Consider:

* ARV installation
* Vault construction
* Future maintenance
* Future replacement

Total Incremental ARV Cost:

ARV Cost = Installation + O&M + Replacement

---

## Finding the Crossover Point

The decision point occurs where:

Additional Deep Profile Cost = Total Lifecycle ARV Cost

Beyond that point, returning to grade may become the more economical option.

Before that point, remaining deep may provide better value.

---

# The Bigger Lesson

Many infrastructure design decisions are not purely hydraulic decisions.

They are lifecycle cost decisions.

The most economical solution is not always the shallowest profile or the deepest profile.

The most economical solution is often the one that balances:

* Construction cost
* Operational cost
* Maintenance burden
* Asset longevity
* Utility risk tolerance

Understanding where those costs intersect is often more valuable than focusing on any single design variable.

For force mains, the real question is rarely whether a high point can be avoided.

The more important question is whether avoiding it creates enough value to justify the cost.
