# What Operators Know That Asset Databases Don't

One thing I've come to appreciate after 25 years in water and wastewater engineering is that not all infrastructure knowledge lives in a database.

Utilities invest significant time and resources maintaining GIS systems, asset management software, maintenance records, and SCADA data. Those systems are essential for understanding what assets we own, where they are located, and how they've been maintained.

But they don't capture everything.

Some of the most valuable information about a system exists only in the experience of the people who operate and maintain it every day.

Operators know things like:

- Pump #2 has developed a vibration that doesn't show up in maintenance records.
- That check valve has been sticking intermittently for years.
- A pressure sensor tends to drift on hot summer afternoons.
- One section of force main is always the first to trap air after maintenance.
- A lift station behaves differently during heavy rainfall, even though the level sensors appear normal.

These observations are difficult to quantify, but they're often critical to understanding how a system actually performs.

This is one reason I believe infrastructure analytics should complement engineering judgment and operational experience, not replace them.

Analytics can identify trends, detect anomalies, and process more information than any individual could reasonably analyze.

Operators provide the context that explains WHY those patterns exist.

Neither is sufficient on its own.

As utilities continue adopting more data-driven approaches, one challenge will become increasingly important: capturing institutional knowledge before it walks out the door through retirements and workforce transitions.

The future of infrastructure management isn't choosing between experience and data.

It's combining engineering judgment, operational experience, and infrastructure analytics to make better decisions.
