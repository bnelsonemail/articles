# Lessons Learned from 25 Years in Water and Wastewater Engineering

When I graduated, I assumed engineering was mostly about calculations.

If I could perform the hydraulics correctly, follow the standards, and produce quality plans, I thought good projects would naturally follow.

Twenty-five years later, I still believe calculations matter.

They're just not what determines whether a project succeeds.

The biggest lessons I've learned have come from job sites, conversations with operators, difficult design decisions, and watching projects get built.

Here are a few that have stayed with me.

## Field observations matter more than assumptions

A drawing only tells part of the story.

Existing utilities aren't always where they're shown. Site conditions rarely match expectations perfectly. Contractors often find problems no one anticipated during design.

Some of the best engineering decisions I've made started with simply walking the project site.

## Good design isn't maximizing one objective

Early in my career, I tended to optimize for the design calculation.

Now I spend much more time thinking about construction, maintenance, operations, and lifecycle cost.

A technically correct solution isn't always the best solution.

## Communication is part of engineering

Some of the most important work happens outside the calculations.

Explaining tradeoffs to clients.

Listening to operators.

Working through concerns with contractors.

Helping junior engineers understand *why* a decision was made instead of simply telling them what to draw.

Clear communication prevents as many problems as good design.

## Experience improves judgment

Design manuals provide answers.

Experience helps you recognize which questions need to be asked first.

Over time, engineering becomes less about solving textbook problems and more about balancing competing priorities with incomplete information.

## Never Stop Learning

One thing that has surprised me over the past 25 years is how much engineering continues to evolve.

The fundamentals haven't changed. Hydraulics, sound design practices, and engineering judgment remain just as important today as they were when I started my career.

What has changed are the tools available to help us make better decisions.

That's one of the reasons I've spent the last few years learning software development, data analytics, and applied machine learning. These aren't skills I've relied on throughout my engineering career. They're skills I'm intentionally developing because I believe they'll play an increasingly important role in how we design, operate, and manage infrastructure.

I don't see technology replacing engineering judgment.

I see it giving engineers better information to apply that judgment.

After 25 years, I've learned that the profession never stops changing. The best engineers aren't the ones who know everything. They're the ones who continue learning long after they become experienced.
