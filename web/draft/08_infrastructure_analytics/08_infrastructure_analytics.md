# Utilities Don't Have a Data Problem. They Have a Decision Problem.

## Introduction

Over the past 25 years, I've worked on projects involving water distribution systems, wastewater collection systems, lift stations, force mains, and treatment facilities.

Looking back, I've realized that many of the most difficult engineering decisions had very little to do with performing calculations.

The calculations were often straightforward.

The difficult part was deciding where limited resources would produce the greatest benefit.

Utilities rarely have enough funding, personnel, or time to address every concern. Every rehabilitation project, inspection, replacement program, and maintenance activity competes for those same limited resources.

Engineering has always been about making the best possible decisions with imperfect information.

Today, however, the amount of information available is changing dramatically.

---

## Engineering Has Always Been a Decision-Making Profession

When people think about engineering, they often picture calculations, design standards, and technical specifications.

Those are certainly important, but many of the most challenging decisions occur long before any calculations begin.

Every utility constantly faces questions such as:

- Which water mains should be replaced first?
- Which lift stations deserve rehabilitation?
- Which force mains present the greatest operational risk?
- Where should leak detection efforts be concentrated?
- Which pump station should receive limited maintenance funding this year?
- Which capital projects will provide the greatest long-term benefit?

These are not simply engineering calculations.

They are resource allocation decisions made under uncertainty.

Every decision involves balancing risk, cost, operational reliability, and limited budgets.

---

## Traditional Sources of Engineering Information

Historically, utilities have relied on a combination of engineering judgment and operational experience to make these decisions.

Information has typically come from sources such as:

- Operator observations
- Maintenance records
- Customer complaints
- Water main break history
- CCTV inspections
- Field investigations
- SCADA alarms
- Routine inspections
- Engineering experience

These sources remain incredibly valuable.

In fact, I don't believe they will ever be replaced.

However, they also have limitations.

Many infrastructure problems develop gradually over months or even years. Small operational changes often go unnoticed because they don't immediately trigger alarms or generate customer complaints.

By the time a problem becomes obvious, the underlying issue may have been developing for quite some time.

---

## What's Different Today?

One of the biggest changes occurring across the water industry is the sheer volume of operational data now being collected.

Utilities increasingly monitor systems through:

- SCADA systems
- Pressure sensors
- Flow meters
- Tank level instrumentation
- Pump telemetry
- Energy monitoring
- Smart meters
- Water quality instrumentation

These systems generate enormous amounts of information every day.

The opportunity isn't simply collecting more data.

The opportunity is understanding what that data is telling us about how our infrastructure behaves.

---

## Data Alone Doesn't Solve Problems

Collecting data does not automatically improve decision making.

In fact, many organizations have access to tremendous amounts of operational data while still struggling to answer practical engineering questions.

Questions like:

- Which pressure zones deserve additional investigation?
- Which pump is beginning to lose efficiency?
- Which lift station is operating differently than it did six months ago?
- Which transmission main deserves inspection first?
- Which assets appear to present the greatest operational risk?

The challenge isn't generating data.

The challenge is transforming data into actionable engineering insight.

Data only becomes valuable when it helps engineers make better decisions.

---

## Where Infrastructure Analytics Fits

This is where infrastructure analytics begins to provide real value.

Rather than replacing engineering judgment, analytics complements it.

Engineering experience remains essential for understanding system behavior, evaluating alternatives, and making final decisions.

Analytics provides another layer of information that can help engineers recognize patterns that would otherwise remain hidden.

This may involve techniques such as:

- Statistical analysis
- Trend analysis
- Time-series analysis
- Risk modeling
- Predictive analytics
- Machine learning

The goal isn't to allow algorithms to make engineering decisions.

The goal is to give engineers better information before making those decisions.

---

## Reducing Uncertainty

One of the biggest misconceptions surrounding predictive analytics is the idea that it can predict the future with certainty.

It can't.

Engineering has always involved uncertainty.

Infrastructure analytics doesn't eliminate uncertainty.

It helps reduce it.

By combining engineering knowledge with operational data, statistical analysis, and predictive techniques, we can improve our understanding of system behavior and make decisions with greater confidence.

Sometimes that means identifying an emerging operational issue earlier.

Sometimes it means confirming that a suspected problem isn't actually developing.

Both outcomes improve decision making.

## Why This Interests Me

One of the reasons I've spent so much time studying statistics, Python, and machine learning is because I believe these disciplines will become increasingly valuable to practicing engineers.

I don't see them replacing engineering judgment.

I see them helping engineers better understand increasingly complex infrastructure systems, recognize patterns hidden within operational data, and make more informed decisions.

To me, this isn't a departure from engineering.

It's simply another tool that helps engineers solve the same problems we've always been trying to solve.

---

## Conclusion

Utilities don't have a data problem.

They have a decision problem.

As utilities continue collecting more operational data, the opportunity isn't simply monitoring systems.

It's transforming operational data into engineering insight that helps reduce uncertainty, prioritize limited resources, and improve infrastructure decision making.

Infrastructure analytics isn't about predicting the future with certainty.

It's about reducing uncertainty so engineers can make better decisions.

Ultimately, better decisions lead to more reliable infrastructure, more efficient use of public resources, and better service for the communities we serve.
